About

Policy-relevant research grounded in measurement

My research sits at the intersection of food economics, consumer behavior, and public policy. I study how households make spending and time-use decisions, and how economic conditions, market structure, and behavioral factors shape food demand, diet, and health outcomes.

I lead work on the Food Expenditure Series (FES), integrating data across Federal sources to measure national and State-level food spending. I also conduct micro-level empirical research using applied econometric methods and survey data to analyze food choices, time use, and nutrition.

My work further examines food markets and industry structure, including retail concentration, competition, online grocery adoption, and how commuting and remote work reshape urban food demand. Across projects, I aim to improve measurement and provide policy-relevant insights into how consumers and markets interact within the food system.

Data & Methods

  • Data development and curation: Food Expenditure Series
  • Survey design and implementation: Eating and Health Module of the American Time Usu Survey
  • Administrative and survey data integration: BLS, BEA, Census, and realted Federal data sources
  • Microdata for empirical analysis: NHANES, NETS, SafeGraph, CEX
  • Methods: Panel data, time series, and cross-sectional econometrics (FE, DiD, decomposition methods)
  • Applied policy analysis and data visualization

Selected Research

Research themes

A curated view of my main research contributions. The full publication record follows below.

Food Expenditure Measurement

Leading the development and expansion of the Food Expenditure Series (FES), integrating data across Federal sources to measure national and State-level food spending. This work advances how food expenditures are tracked across outlets, products, and consumers, improving measurement for policy and research.

Time Use, Eating, and Health

Designing and using the Eating and Health Module (EHM) of the American Time Use Survey to study how individuals allocate time to eating, grocery shopping, meal preparation. This work examines how daily behaviors, timing, and context shape diet, health outcomes, and consumer decision-making.

Food Markets and Consumer Behavior

Studying food markets and industry structure, including retail concentration, online grocery adoption, food-away-from-home demand. This works combines high-frequency, large-scale mobility and establishment data with microdata and applied econometrics to analyze how economic shocks and changes in workplace behavior reshape local food markets and consumer behavior.

Working Papers

Submitted and in-progress research

Population Mobility and State Food Spending: Distinguishing Resident Demand from Visitor Activity

Develops mobility-adjusted measures of State-level food spending to better distinguish resident demand from tourism and commuting effects.

Submitted / under review

Workplace Location and the Spatial Redistribution of Restaurant Demand

with Brandon Restrepo

Quantifies how remote and hybrid work reduced downtown restaurant demand, using mobility data to show a persistent shift away from central business districts.

Submitted / under review

Estimating Food Expenditure Smoothing in the United States: The Role of Transfers

with Ergys Islamaj

Examines how households smooth food spending over income shocks, highlighting the role of government transfers in stabilizing expenditures and reducing food insecurity.

In progress

Eating in Combination: Decomposing the Role of Daily Eating Patterns in U.S. Obesity

Examines how combinations of daily eating patterns relate to obesity, using time-use data to identify which behaviors jointly contribute most to differences in BMI.
