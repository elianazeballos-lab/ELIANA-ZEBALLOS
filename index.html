Agricultural economist • Food expenditures • Consumer behavior

Eliana Zeballos, PhD

Applied economist specializing in food expenditures, consumer behavior, time use, and food market dynamics.

I conduct policy-relevant research using large-scale administrative, survey, and emerging data sources. My work focuses on how U.S. consumers allocate time and money to food, how food markets evolve, and how better measurement can improve public understanding of the food system.

Selected Research

Research themes

A curated view of my main research contributions. The full publication record follows below.

Food Expenditure Measurement

Leading and expanding the Food Expenditure Series, including State-level estimates and new approaches to measuring food spending across outlets, products, and purchasers.

Time Use, Eating, and Health

Using the American Time Use Survey and Eating and Health Module to understand how adults allocate time to eating, grocery shopping, meal preparation, and health-related behaviors.

Food Markets and Consumer Behavior

Studying food retail concentration, online grocery adoption, food-away-from-home spending, and how economic shocks affect food demand.

Submitted and in-progress research

Understanding State-Level Disparities in Food-Away-From-Home Expenditures: The Role of Commuting and Tourism

with Anne Byrne and Wilson Sinclair

Examines how commuting and tourism affect State-level food-away-from-home spending and uses mobility-based evidence to distinguish local sales from resident-based demand.

Submitted / under review

Missing the Lunch Rush: Downtown Workforce and the Food-Away-From-Home Environment

Uses mobility and establishment data to assess how workplace presence shapes downtown foodservice activity and restaurant demand.

Submitted / under review

Measuring Food Expenditure Smoothing in the U.S.: The Role of Transfers

with Ergys Islamaj and Wilson Sinclair

Analyzes how transfers help smooth food expenditures during economic downturns and other periods of financial stress.

About

Policy-relevant research grounded in measurement

My research sits at the intersection of food economics, consumer behavior, and public policy. I focus on how consumers allocate resources to food and how economic shocks, market structure, and time constraints shape food choices.

I lead work on the Food Expenditure Series and co-manage the Eating and Health Module of the American Time Use Survey. Across projects, I prioritize clear measurement, rigorous empirical methods, and accessible communication for policy audiences.

Data & Methods

  • Food Expenditure Series
  • American Time Use Survey / Eating and Health Module
  • BLS, NETS, SafeGraph, and other administrative or commercial data
  • Panel data, time series, cross-sectional econometrics
  • Applied policy analysis and data visualization
